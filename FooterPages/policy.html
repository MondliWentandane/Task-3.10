Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)

A Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) is a process designed to identify and minimize risks associated with the processing of personal data. It's a critical tool for risk negation and demonstrating GDPR compliance.

Benefits of Conducting a DPIA:

  • Ensures GDPR Compliance: Helps organizations meet their legal obligations and avoid sanctions.
  • Builds Public Confidence: Improves transparency and communication about data protection issues.
  • Protects User Rights: Safeguards individuals from data protection rights violations.
  • Enables "Data Protection by Design": Integrates data privacy features and technologies into project design from the early stages, making protection more effective and cost-efficient.
  • Data protection by design: Embedding privacy from the outset.
  • Data protection by default: Service settings are automatically privacy-friendly.
  • Reduces Operational Costs: Optimizes information flows, eliminating unnecessary data collection and processing.
  • Reduces Organizational Risks: Proactively addresses data protection risks.
  • Cost-Effective Safeguards: Integrating safeguards early reduces later costs and disruptions.

When is a DPIA Mandatory (GDPR)?

A DPIA is mandatory under GDPR when data processing is "likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons." This is especially relevant for new technologies. Even when not strictly mandatory, conducting a DPIA is considered good practice.

Examples of "High Risk" Processing (requiring a DPIA):

  • Systematic and Extensive Evaluation/Profiling: Processing that involves automated evaluation of personal aspects (including profiling) leading to decisions that have legal or similarly significant effects on individuals.
    Examples: Banks screening customers for credit, biotechnology companies offering genetic tests to predict health risks, companies building behavioral marketing profiles.
  • Large Scale Processing of Special Categories of Data or Criminal Convictions/Offences: Processing a significant volume of sensitive data or data relating to criminal convictions.
    Examples: A large hospital keeping patient medical records, a private investigator storing offender details. This also includes electronic communication data, location data, or financial data that could increase risk.
  • Systematic Monitoring of a Publicly Accessible Area on a Large Scale: Observing, monitoring, or controlling data subjects, especially in public spaces.
    Example: Extensive CCTV surveillance systems in public areas.

Criteria for Assessing High Risk (from Article 29 Working Party):

  • Evaluation or Scoring: Profiling and predicting aspects like work performance, economic situation, health, preferences, reliability, behavior, location, or movements.
  • Automated Decision-Making with Legal or Similar Significant Effect: Decisions that produce legal effects or significantly affect individuals, potentially leading to exclusion or discrimination.
  • Systematic Monitoring: Observing, monitoring, or controlling data subjects, especially in public areas.
  • Sensitive Data: Processing special categories of data (Article 9), criminal convictions/offenses data (Article 10), or other data types (like communication, location, financial) that increase risk. Even personal or household activity data stored in cloud services can be high-risk if its disclosure would be intrusive.
